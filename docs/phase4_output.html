Objective:
To evaluate the economic and logistical feasibility of a global transition to a nutritionally adequate, plant-based food system. This includes examining cost structures, infrastructure readiness, supply chain dependencies, and transition investment requirements.

Scope & Method:
Phase 4 includes four modules: comparative production costs and elasticity across plant and animal food categories (Module 1), labor and infrastructure readiness by region (Module 2), global trade dependencies and processing chokepoints (Module 3), and investment scenarios for food system transitions (Module 4). Evidence is drawn from FAOStat, OECD, WFP, IMF, and national economic reports. Deliverables include synthesis texts (TX), data tables (DT), and metadata registers.

Key Findings:

  • Cost Efficiency: Cereal, legume, and tuber production is significantly less expensive per kilocalorie than animal products. Subsidies continue to distort market costs.
  • Labor Input: Plant-based food systems require less labor per kcal but need expanded post-harvest and processing infrastructure in the Global South.
  • Trade Chokepoints: High reliance on a few regions for processing (e.g., East Asia, Northern Europe) introduces risk. Critical corridors (e.g., Suez, Panama) pose vulnerability.
  • Transition Investment: Transitioning selected countries (e.g., Brazil, India) would require $150â€“$600 per capita. Public finance currently undercovers required investment.

Implications:
Phase 4 confirms that a plant-based food system is economically viable under current conditions but will require targeted infrastructure investment, supply chain resilience planning, and financial support mechanisms to address regional disparities. These findings provide an empirical foundation for political and cultural feasibility assessment in Phase 5.

Module 1

Module 1 â€“ Agricultural Economics

This text analyzes cost structures of plant-based versus animal-based food categories using peer-reviewed agricultural economic data. Regional differentiation is included to account for labor, input costs, and production efficiencies. Price volatility is discussed using 10-year FAO index data, with attention to subsidy mechanisms affecting real market prices.

Key findings include:

  • Lower average production cost per kilocalorie for legumes, cereals, and tubers compared to beef, dairy, and poultry.
  • Subsidies for animal products distort true cost comparisons.
  • High elasticity observed in soy, wheat, and maize supply chains.

Sources: FAO, OECD Agricultural Outlook, IFPRI data (ðŸŸ¢).

Module 2

Module 2 â€“ Labor & Infrastructure Requirements

This module evaluates regional labor intensity and infrastructural readiness for plant-based food systems. It utilizes FAO labor input datasets and World Bank infrastructure readiness indices.

Findings:

  • Labor inputs for legume and cereal cultivation are significantly lower per kcal than animal husbandry.
  • Processing infrastructure for legumes and grains is more evenly distributed than cold-chain systems for meat and dairy.
  • Major infrastructure gaps exist in sub-Saharan Africa and parts of Southeast Asia.

Sources: FAO labor data, World Bank logistics index (ðŸŸ¢).

Module 3

Module 3 â€“ Trade & Supply Chain Dependencies

This module maps global trade flows for key plant-based staples, intermediary processing requirements, and vulnerability points. Analysis is grounded in COMTRADE and FAOSTAT data, supported by WFP logistics maps.

Findings:

  • Soy, wheat, and maize dominate plant-based export flows.
  • Processing chokepoints are regionally concentrated in East Asia and Northern Europe.
  • Supply chains are vulnerable to transport corridor disruptions (e.g., Suez, Panama).

Sources: UN COMTRADE, FAOSTAT, WFP Atlas (ðŸŸ¢).

Module 4

Module 4 â€“ Transition Investment and Costs

This module evaluates the economic cost of converting from a mixed food system to a plant-based one. It includes analysis of capital expenditure, public/private financing sources, and investment gaps.

Case studies include Brazil, India, and the EU.

Key findings:

  • Transition investment varies widely: $150â€“$600 per capita depending on baseline infrastructure.
  • Public financing currently covers <40% of estimated needs.
  • Most cost-effective transitions involve retooling existing cereal infrastructure.

Sources: FAO Investment Centre, IMF GFSR, IPES-Food (ðŸŸ¡).
