Objective:
To assess the socio-cultural and political feasibility of shifting to a global plant-based food system. The phase focuses on identifying enabling conditions and resistance across cultural identities, political structures, legal environments, and public narratives.

Scope & Method:
Phase 5 comprises four modules: dietary identity and cultural foodways (Module 1), political economy and agribusiness lobbying (Module 2), legal and regulatory constraints (Module 3), and public discourse and media framing (Module 4). Data sources include Pew Research, FAOLEX, WTO notifications, and IPES-Food media analysis. Deliverables include synthesis texts (TX), region-specific indicator datasets (DT), and compliance metadata.

Key Findings:

  • Cultural Framing: Deep-rooted cultural identities shape dietary choices, but many traditions already support or accommodate plant-based foods.
  • Political Resistance: Institutional inertia and lobbying from legacy meat/dairy sectors slow transition efforts, particularly in the US and EU.
  • Legal Barriers: Food labeling restrictions and subsidy imbalances persist, although international norms (e.g., WTO) are increasingly scrutinized.
  • Public Trust: Effective framing (e.g., health/climate) outperforms ethical appeals. Trust in food authorities varies sharply by region.

Implications:
Cultural and political factors represent critical determinants in the pace and nature of dietary transitions. While barriers exist, alignment of public messaging, institutional support, and culturally resonant framings can significantly enhance acceptability. The findings inform scenario design and stakeholder mapping in Phase 6.

Module 1

Module 1 â€“ Cultural Norms and Dietary Identity

This module analyzes the cultural embeddedness of animal-based diets and the role of plant-based foodways across civilizations. It draws on historical anthropological studies, religious dietary laws, and recent behavioral surveys.

Findings:

  • High cultural attachment to meat in pastoralist societies and Western industrial contexts.
  • Major religious traditions (e.g., Hinduism, Jainism, Seventh-day Adventism) embed plant-based principles.
  • Surveys in Europe and Asia suggest moderate willingness to reduce meat if cultural identity is respected.

Sources: Pew Research (2021), FAO cultural food heritage reports, ethnographic data (ðŸŸ¢).

Module 2

Module 2 â€“ Political Economy and Power Structures

This module assesses the role of agribusiness lobbying, institutional inertia, and regulatory capture in shaping the food system. The analysis uses lobbying expenditure records, documented cases of policy resistance, and positions of global food institutions.

Findings:

  • Major meat and dairy lobbies dominate food policy advisory boards in North America and Europe.
  • Regulatory inertia is reinforced by subsidy entrenchment and rural constituency pressures.
  • FAO shows increasing openness to plant-based transitions, while USDA and CAP retain legacy biases.

Sources: EU CAP Reports, OpenSecrets.org, FAO Food Systems Summit Proceedings (ðŸŸ¢/ðŸŸ¡).

Module 3

Module 3 â€“ Regulatory and Legal Constraints

This module surveys national policies, food labeling regulations, and legal frameworks that influence the viability of plant-based food systems. Focus is placed on subsidy structures, trade classification, and food sovereignty debates.

Findings:

  • Over 60 countries subsidize animal agriculture more heavily than plant production.
  • Legal naming restrictions on plant-based products exist in the EU, USA, and Brazil.
  • Trade classifications under WTO rules create inconsistencies in tariff treatment.

Sources: WTO trade database, national subsidy registries, FAOLEX legal database (ðŸŸ¢).

Module 4

Module 4 â€“ Public Communication and Framing

This module evaluates how plant-based diets are portrayed in media, the effectiveness of public campaigns, and the influence of misinformation. It also examines public trust in food authorities and educational system engagement.

Findings:

  • Frames emphasizing climate benefits and health impacts are more persuasive than ethical appeals.
  • Opposition narratives center on cultural threat and "fake food" tropes, amplified by industry actors.
  • Public trust in nutrition authorities varies widely; highest in Scandinavia, lowest in North America.

Sources: Media Content Analysis (Lancet Planet Health), IPES-Food, national trust surveys (ðŸŸ¡).
