Objective:
To test the robustness, generalizability, and potential failure modes of prior findings (Phases 1â€“6), including underlying assumptions, variable sensitivities, domain limitations, and known counterarguments.

Scope & Method:
Phase 7 is organized across four modules: assumption audits and model sensitivity (Module 1), evidence gaps and empirical contradictions (Module 2), external risks and system fragilities (Module 3), and cross-disciplinary critique and dissent (Module 4). Sources include IPCC, FAO, peer-reviewed critiques, and institutional data divergences.

Key Findings:

  • Modeling Assumptions: Key variables such as yield, substitution ratios, and nutrient bioavailability carry wide sensitivity ranges and idealized assumptions.
  • Data Conflicts: Conflicting institutional data (e.g., FAO vs. WB) challenge uniform surplus or undernourishment claims.
  • Systemic Risks: Climate instability, trade chokepoints, and infrastructure inertia pose credible threats to a smooth transition.
  • Dissent: Political ecologists and rural economists raise valid concerns around sovereignty, equity, and top-down reform risks.

Implications:
The transition to a plant-based global food system, while plausible and beneficial in aggregate, is highly contingent on uncertain parameters, region-specific risks, and pluralistic social realities. These critiques and failure modes will inform the final synthesis and scenario testing in Phase 8.

Module 1

Module 1 â€“ Assumptions and Model Dependencies

This module critically examines foundational assumptions in Phases 1â€“3, including nutrient adequacy models, substitution rules, and land/emissions forecasts.

Findings:

  • Nutrient targets assume ideal bioavailability and standard fortification regimes, which vary significantly by region and demographic.
  • Yield forecasts used median historical trends; sensitivity to input variability (e.g., fertilizer, climate) was only partially modeled.
  • Substitution logic (e.g., replacing dairy with legumes) lacks behavioral nuance and ignores preparation feasibility in some regions.

Sources: Global Diet Quality Project, EAT-Lancet critiques, IPCC scenario range studies (ðŸŸ¢/ðŸŸ¡).

Module 2

Module 2 â€“ Evidence Gaps and Contradictions

This module identifies empirical gaps and data contradictions across the evidence base from Phases 1â€“6.

Findings:

  • No standardized measurement of soil regeneration under different crop rotations across regions.
  • FAO and World Bank disagree on estimates of global undernourishment and food waste, affecting surplus projections.
  • Regional dietary acceptability data (e.g., indigenous populations, urban poor) is sparse or outdated in major data sources.

Sources: FAO SOFI 2023, World Bank Food Security Tracker, peer-reviewed reviews (ðŸŸ¢).

Module 3

Module 3 â€“ External Risks and Shocks

This module evaluates exogenous risks that could compromise or reverse the transition to plant-based food systems, including trade shocks, climate events, and systemic lock-ins.

Findings:

  • Climate shocks (e.g., multiyear droughts, pest outbreaks) could destabilize monoculture-dependent regions.
  • Trade chokepoints (e.g., Suez, Panama) remain critical for staple flows in high-transition scenarios.
  • Path dependency from food infrastructure investment may lock in suboptimal dietary or processing patterns.

Sources: IPCC Risk Atlas, UNCTAD Trade Vulnerability Index, FAO climateâ€“food interface reports (ðŸŸ¢).

Module 4

Module 4 â€“ Cross-Disciplinary Critique and Dissent

This module documents structured dissent from academic and stakeholder sources, focusing on critiques from political ecology, anthropology, rural studies, and economic modeling.

Findings:

  • Anthropologists argue that top-down dietary shifts often ignore food sovereignty and cultural resilience.
  • Political ecology critiques warn of "green colonialism" if global models override local priorities.
  • Rural economic studies suggest high transitional risk for livestock-dependent communities without compensation guarantees.

Sources: Rural Sociology (2022), Journal of Peasant Studies (2023), stakeholder analyses (ðŸŸ¡).
